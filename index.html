AIDA

Aid Information and Data Architecture — designing the bridge between raw IATI XML and the humans who need to understand, publish, and act on global aid data. Six distinct user types. Two product modes. One coherent platform.

Organisation

Zimmerman BV, Amsterdam

My Role

Lead UX/UI Designer

Timeline

2023 – Present

Scope

End-to-end, 0→1

01 — The Problem

Transparency existed. Usability didn't.

IATI has succeeded in making aid data publicly available — but being available is not the same as being usable. The data lives in structured XML that requires specialist querying skills to extract meaning from. NGOs face high friction producing compliant files. Policy analysts and field workers can't get answers without technical help.

The organisations most responsible for aid accountability were the least equipped to work with the raw data format designed to hold them accountable.

AIDA's challenge was to serve both sides simultaneously: lower the publishing barrier for those contributing data, and make that same data explorable and interpretable for those consuming it — without building two disconnected products.

02 — Two Products, One Platform

Explorer & Publisher — tightly coupled.

AIDA delivers through two product modes that live under one roof. The design challenge was creating a unified information architecture that doesn't fragment users who genuinely need both — especially dual-mode users who publish their own data and explore others'.

AIDA Explorer

Use & insight

Interactive dashboards for IATI data — funding totals, recipient geographies, organisations, sectors, and activity-level views. Powerful filtering with featured filter presets to guide non-technical users into meaningful exploration without the blank-canvas problem.

AIDA Publisher

Publish & compliance

A guided publication journey that abstracts IATI XML into a non-frightening 3-step workflow. Connect → Validate → Publish. Templates and Gold Standard files reduce friction for first-time publishers while preserving full spec compliance underneath.

Explorer — overview
Explorer — locations
Explorer — organisations

The Publisher abstracts a technically demanding standard into three clear steps — removing the blank-canvas problem with templates, and surfacing errors inline with fix suggestions before anything reaches the IATI Registry.

01

Connect your data

Upload CSV, XLSX or XML — single or batch. Connect Google Drive or OneDrive. Gold Standard templates remove the blank-canvas problem for first-time publishers.

02

Validate & visualise

Check for errors, review data previews and validity status. Clear error messages with fix suggestions. Pre-publication data visualisations build trust before submission.

03

Publish to IATI

Connect the IATI Registry once with your Publisher ID and API Token. Select files, submit, and track publishing status — Drafted → Published → Visualised.

03 — Users

Through participatory research and stakeholder sessions, I mapped six distinct user archetypes — each with different data literacy, sector focus, and relationship to IATI. The design challenge was finding shared mental models that could support all of them without dumbing down the power interface for experts.

04 — Design Challenges

The hard problems I had to solve.

1
One platform, two completely different mental modelsPublishers think in terms of compliance, files, and deadlines. Explorers think in terms of questions, filters, and stories. The navigation and information architecture had to honour both without creating two disconnected products or confusing single-mode users.
2
Surfacing the right depth for each user typeA UN analyst needs drill-down to activity level. A policy maker needs a one-page executive summary. The same underlying data had to power both — decided at the filter and dashboard layer, not by building separate views per persona.
3
Making IATI compliance non-frighteningFor publishers like Amina, existing IATI tooling is a wall of technical jargon and XML schemas. I had to abstract the standard into a guided workflow while preserving full spec compliance underneath — and build trust through clear validation feedback before any data leaves the system.
4
Designing for low data literacy without dumbing downAIDA's users span from field NGO coordinators to data scientists. Every feature needed a clear entry point for novices while remaining powerful enough for experts — featured filters guide newcomers into meaningful exploration without cluttering the advanced UI for power users.
05 — Key Design Decisions

Where the real design thinking happened.

→ Chosen

Unified platform with role-aware entry points

Explorer and Publisher share a single nav and design system. Users self-select their mode but can switch freely — essential for dual-mode users like Dr. Alvarez and Amina. Publishers also benefit from seeing their data visualised post-publish, reinforcing compliance motivation.

Alternative considered

Two separate products

Cleaner separation, but would fragment users who genuinely need both modes and create double the maintenance surface for the design system and component library.

→ Chosen

Progressive disclosure in publishing flow

3-step mental model (Connect → Validate → Publish) hides XML complexity. Templates and Gold Standard files solve the blank-canvas problem. Inline validation with fix suggestions builds trust before data leaves the system.

Alternative considered

Full-form XML editor

Maximum fidelity, but alienating for the majority of publishers. Reserved as an advanced mode for power users rather than the default entry point.

→ Chosen

Featured filters for guided discovery

Curated filter presets guide non-technical users into meaningful data views immediately. Reduces the paradox of choice when a dataset has dozens of filterable dimensions. Power filters remain accessible — just not the default landing state.

Alternative considered

Fully open filter panel

More powerful at first glance, but user testing showed it paralysed less experienced users. They didn't know where to start when facing dozens of filter options simultaneously.

06 — Design System

Building for scale across a portfolio of platforms.

AIDA doesn't exist in isolation — it shares a design system with The Global Fund Data Explorer and several other aid-data portals at Zimmerman BV. I established and maintain the component library in Figma, including data visualisation components (ECharts-based), filtering patterns, and accessibility tokens that meet WCAG AA throughout.

The version 3 redesign — including the logo, typography (Prompt Bold + Inter), and colour system — was designed to be legible and contemporary across all interfaces.

What the design achieved.

2

08 — Reflections

What I'd carry into the next project.

1

Dual-mode users are your most valuable research subjects. Dr. Alvarez's use of both Explorer and Publisher in the same session revealed information architecture tensions that single-mode personas never surfaced. Recruit for the edge case.

2

Complexity doesn't always need to be hidden — it needs to be timed. Progressive disclosure worked not by removing hard decisions, but by sequencing them when users had enough context to make them confidently. The XML is still there; it just arrives later.

3

In civic tech, trust is a design element. Users publishing official government data need to feel the system is robust before they'll commit. Validation feedback, pre-publish previews, and clear error states weren't nice-to-haves — they were load-bearing features for adoption.

4

A shared design system compounds your impact. Building the component library to scale across AIDA and other Zimmerman portals meant design decisions improved platform-wide, not just locally. The investment pays back exponentially over time.

FoodFlow

A digital service platform that supports food banks and food aid NGOs in forecasting, prospecting, and managing donations — reducing uncertainty and improving nutritional variety in food aid.

Problem

Food-aid instability and lack of nutritional variation in donations

Solution

Data-driven platform to forecast, share, and manage food donations between NGOs

My contributions

Design Research · UX & UI Design · Brand Communication

Team

Aylin Paçacı, Beatrice Fidone, Carolina Iplinsky, Giovanna Mundstock, Juliana Maines, Jun Deng

Status quo in food aid NGOs

Food banks and food-aid NGOs face deep uncertainties with donations — compromising planning, response speed, and the consistency of food available for distribution. This unpredictability affects the health and wellbeing of those in need, creating an urgent need for more effective systems for managing donations, reducing waste, and ensuring organisations have the resources they need.

Field research — visits to food banks and NGOs in Milan

Aim & vision

The goal is to improve the variety and stability of food donations by optimising resources — unifying inventory databases across NGOs to identify excesses or shortages, enabling exchanges, and optimising aid distribution for all.

Our solution

FoodFlow provides two services: a surplus food sharing network for food banks and NGOs, and an inventory management system. The network enables exchange of surplus food between organisations, while the software tracks inventory, identifies shortages, and plans deliveries. A mobile app lets volunteers manage product information on the go; a desktop app gives logistic managers full oversight.

Service details — ecosystem & offering maps

To develop FoodFlow, we used an ecosystem map to understand internal and external factors and place the service in context. The offering map identifies products and services and how they connect to meet target audiences and uncover areas for improvement.

App information architecture

The FoodFlow app is the service's primary touchpoint. We built a detailed information architecture using ontology and taxonomy tools before moving into screen design — ensuring every structural decision was grounded in how NGOs actually categorise and manage food.

Prototype walkthrough

A walkthrough of the FoodFlow mobile app prototype — demonstrating the core flows for managing inventory, tracking active donations, and forecasting future aid.

High fidelity screens

The mobile app enables volunteers to manage food inventory in real time. The desktop Cupboard interface gives logistic managers full oversight: stock levels, donation forecasting, nutritional balance tracking, and expiry monitoring.

ENI One

A yearly internal platform that celebrates genuine feedback and employee effort within ENI — paired with an annual event that brings the spirit of recognition to life.

Context

Politecnico di Milano

Duration

12 weeks

Period

Feb – Jun 2022

My Role

Design Research, UX Design

The problem

"The Great Resignation" — many employees were quitting due to a lack of meaningful recognition and feedback. Remote work intensified this: employees felt disconnected and unappreciated, especially in large, complex organisations like ENI.

01Employees need to feel seen and confident in what they do
02Genuine feedback increases job satisfaction significantly
03Work recognition and job satisfaction are directly correlated
04Positive feedback is what employees remember most after completing a project

Co-design with ENI employees

We ran co-design sessions directly with ENI employees — a collaborative approach that gave them a direct say in the project's development. Key insight: employees deeply valued celebrating failures as much as wins, because they care about growth.

Our solution

ENIOne is a platform where employees can see their contributions on projects at scale, track them, hand completed tasks to colleagues, and give and receive feedback. The goal is to stimulate genuine appreciation, create a spillover effect, and contribute to long-term employee motivation.

Context

The status quo in 2037

Modern education has followed the same model for over 300 years. But growing internet access, improvements in technology, and distributed living are forcing a shift. Students increasingly focus on skills and tools over knowledge transmission alone — creating a need for experiential, global, community-rooted learning.

The Glocal service-system

A service that assists students throughout their educational travel journey — from choosing their curriculum, to receiving real-time information on the train (lectures, weather forecasts, upcoming stops), to tracking their learning route and building community with fellow nomadic students.
